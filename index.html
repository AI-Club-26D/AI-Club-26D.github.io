INSEAD AI Club 26D / Manifesto

Nobody knows
the ceiling.

Every previous technology shift had visible limits. Phones can't fly. Fibre can't reach everywhere. With AI, we genuinely don't know what it'll be capable of in two years. That uncertainty is the point. And most people - including most MBA students - are dramatically underestimating how fast this moves.

Written by Freddie Chambers and the 26D AI Club founding team. Backed by primary INSEAD research across 500+ MBA students, conversations with Multiverse's Chief Learning Officer, and published frameworks from Anthropic, Harvard Business School, and Benedict Evans.

01 / The Platform Shift

This is PCs, the web, and mobile.
All over again.

Benedict Evans frames it cleanly: AI is a platform shift on the scale of PCs, the web, and smartphones. Each created entirely new value chains, new gatekeepers, new winners. But the first movers almost never captured the value. It went to the people who built real products and distribution. Not the people who demoed well.

We're in the absorption phase. A third of enterprises have deployed at least one GenAI solution. Another quarter don't plan to do anything yet. Cloud is "old and boring" but still only covers a third of enterprise workflows. Barcodes took years to deploy - but once done, enabled managing 5-10x more SKUs. The real disruption blends into the fabric and becomes unremarkable.

$400bn
Annual capex from major platform companies on AI infrastructure. The consensus: the risk of underinvesting is bigger than the risk of overinvesting.
100M
ChatGPT users in six months. Fastest technology uptake in history. But weekly active usage tells a different story - most people engage infrequently.
<10%
Performance gap between the major LLMs. Models are converging. Distribution and product become the differentiators, not raw intelligence.

But here's what makes this different from every previous platform shift. With PCs, you knew the physical limits. With mobile, you knew the physical limits. With AI, we don't know why these models work so well - so we don't know how much better they're going to get. That uncertainty isn't a footnote. It's the entire story.

"In five years you'll be able to have a million workers on a problem at insignificant cost. A country of geniuses in a datacenter." Dario Amodei, CEO of Anthropic
02 / The Inflection

Nothing done on a screen
is safe in the medium term.

That's not a prediction. It's a description of what's already happening.

Amodei defines powerful AI as "smarter than a Nobel Prize winner across most relevant fields - biology, programming, math, engineering, writing." It can take tasks that take hours, days, or weeks and execute them autonomously. Run millions of instances in parallel, each able to specialise or coordinate. He predicted this could arrive as early as 2026. Many in the industry think he's being conservative.

Matt Shumer puts it more bluntly: AI isn't replacing one specific skill. It's a general substitute for cognitive work. It gets better at everything simultaneously. When factories automated, workers retrained as office workers. When the internet disrupted retail, workers moved into logistics. But AI doesn't leave a convenient gap to move into. Whatever you retrain for, it's improving at that too.

Legal
Reading contracts, summarising case law, drafting briefs at a level that rivals junior associates.
Finance
Financial models, investment memos, data analysis and reports. Competent and improving fast.
Software
A year ago it barely wrote a few lines. Now it writes hundreds of thousands of lines that work correctly.
Medicine
Reading scans, analysing results, suggesting diagnoses. Approaching or exceeding human performance.
Writing
Marketing copy, reports, technical writing. Many professionals can't distinguish it from human work.
Service
AI agents handling complex multi-step customer problems. Not scripts. Actual reasoning.
50%
of entry-level white-collar jobs eliminated within 1-5 years. Amodei's prediction. Many think he's being conservative.
$1.5bn
in generative AI bookings per quarter at Accenture alone. Mostly pilots. The real wave hasn't hit yet.
0
convenient gaps to retrain into. AI improves at everything simultaneously. Whatever you pivot to, it's getting better at that too.

Amodei introduces a new framework for thinking about this: marginal returns to intelligence. Once intelligence is no longer scarce - once you can spin up a million instances - what else limits progress? The answer is physical constraints and real-world timelines. Hardware. Materials. Coordination. The physical world is the one domain where intelligence hits a hard bottleneck. Everything digital is being compressed.

The physical world is the only domain where intelligence hits a hard bottleneck. Everything else is being compressed. Amodei agrees that humans will maintain a relative advantage in the physical world "for a significant time." But he's clear: this is a buffer, not a permanent shield.
03 / The Secret Cyborgs

Half of AI users
never tell anyone.

Ethan Mollick's research is unambiguous: AI boosts individual productivity by 20-70% in controlled studies. But employees keep it secret. For three reasons: organisational bans make disclosure risky. The social advantage of appearing human-authored is real. And the deepest fear - if I show how much AI does, I'm training my own replacement.

At INSEAD the pattern is identical. "The teacher asked who put this through ChatGPT. One person raised their hand - even though everyone did it."

Formal surveys: dramatic under-reporting. Informal conversations: everyone uses AI. That gap is the hidden-use culture. And it's toxic - because the people hiding their AI use aren't learning from each other. They're not sharing workflows, not comparing tools, not building on what works. They're all reinventing the wheel in private.

87%
of students use AI tools for schoolwork. Global survey, 2025. The question isn't whether they use it. It's how.
74%
of corporate recruiters say AI skills are important for business graduates. Nobody's defined what that means.
20-70%
productivity boost from AI use in controlled studies. Yet over half of users never tell their employer. The gains are invisible.

Mollick's prescription: the only way for an organisation to benefit from AI is to get the help of their cyborgs. That means making disclosure safe. Rewarding transparency. Creating environments where sharing your AI workflow is as unremarkable as sharing which spreadsheet app you use.

The hidden-use problem isn't going to solve itself. And the cost of leaving it unsolved is that your best AI practitioners stay invisible while everyone else plateaus.

At Multiverse, AI use is expected. At INSEAD, students hide it. That hidden-use culture is a symptom of being stuck at Productive. When AI feels like a shortcut, it feels like cheating. When it's embedded in your workflow, hiding it would be like hiding that you use email.
04 / The Maturity Gap

You're probably stuck
at "Productive."

Gary Eimerman, Chief Learning Officer at Multiverse, built a four-stage AI maturity model with his learning sciences team. Most corporate training stops at stage one. Most MBA students - including most of this cohort - plateau at stage two.

Literate
You know the vocabulary. You understand prompting. A YouTube tutorial gets you here. Most corporate "AI training" stops at this stage.
Productive
You use AI to do the same things faster. Case prep, summarising, drafting. Same workflows, augmented. This is where most people stop. This is where you probably are.
Automation
You fundamentally rethink how you work. AI becomes a team member you delegate to - not an app you interact with. You stop prompting and start managing.
AI Native
The organisation is built from the ground up with AI as core infrastructure. Nobody's fully here yet. But the bets are on: a one-person company reaching a billion-dollar valuation within five years.

The jump from Productive to Automation is where everything changes. And the barrier isn't technical. It's psychological. People fear making their role replaceable. But everyone who's ever been promoted has already made their previous job replaceable - through hiring, through delegation, through building something better. AI just makes this visible and fast.

What predicts who makes the jump? Not technical background. Not CS degrees. Not engineering experience. Gary asked this directly. The common traits are curiosity - willingness to try new things constantly. Grit - willingness to fail 14 times and get it right on the 15th. And ability to learn - because the tools change every month.

Leadership modelling
Gary personally pushes his EA every other week: "Why aren't you automating that?" When the leader signals it's not just OK but expected, hiding stops.
Incentive structures
Quarterly Founder's Awards for building something impactful with AI. Performance measurement that includes AI adoption behaviours. Cash prizes. Promotions.
Visibility mechanisms
Sharing AI wins on all-hands. Slack engagement on shared automations. Making AI use as unremarkable as spell check. Because spell check is already AI-powered - nobody hides using that.

The contrast with INSEAD is stark. At Multiverse, these levers are working. People share what they build. At INSEAD, the same students who use AI for every assignment will tell a formal survey they barely touch it. That gap is what we're here to close.

"When every option is instantly available, when every variation is possible, the person who knows which one to choose becomes the most valuable person in the room. That skill isn't technical. It's taste." Nitin Nohria, former Dean of Harvard Business School
05 / The Taste Problem

"Good at AI" isn't a technical skill.

INSEAD's Career Development Centre lists "AI & Digital Transformation" as a transferable skill employers want. No definition. No explanation. Just... AI. Tick.

The UK Government spent £4.1m on an AI Skills Hub to upskill 10 million workers. It turned out to be a website linking to courses that don't exist. Four million quid.

The instinct is always to throw training at it. But generative AI is built on mimicking what already exists. It has the power to remix elements and produce plausible new combinations. But it always trends toward average. A 6 out of 10. Always.

Nohria takes this further. AI-generated content is stuffing the world with what he calls "slop" - technically competent but meaningless proliferation. Every product, strategy, or pitch deck can now have dozens of AI-generated versions. The ability to discern which variation is most meaningful is becoming the scarcest and most valuable skill in business.

AI has no taste. It can't tell the difference between something edgy and something offensive. Between something novel and something merely loud. That requires judgment, lived experience, a point of view. None of that shows up on a transferable skills list.

And this isn't a technical shortcoming. It's structural. Taste is born of human discretion - of growing up in particular places, being exposed to particular cultural references, developing a point of view that is inseparable from personality. You can't train a model on taste because taste is the human fingerprint on decision making.

Mollick's "Second Law of AI" reinforces this: experts extract more value from AI than amateurs. They ask better questions. They push deeper with follow-ups. And critically, they catch the errors. As AI errors shift from obvious hallucinations to subtle misjudgments, only people with real domain knowledge can spot what's wrong. The gap between an expert using AI and a novice using AI is widening, not narrowing.

"For all its capabilities, AI has no taste. It cannot originate style with intentionality. It cannot understand why one choice might have emotional resonance while another falls flat." Nitin Nohria, The Atlantic
06 / The Learning Paradox

You can use AI and still learn.
But it requires intention.

Research shows that students who outsource assignments to AI perform on par with students who skipped the assignment entirely. The task gets done. The grade gets awarded. The skill never develops.

Purpose-built AI cheating tools now exist. An app called OpenClaw was built specifically to evade AI detection. The detection arms race is already lost. The solution isn't better policing - it's shifting evaluation to methods that assess the student directly. In-class discussion. Live argument. Oral examination. The kinds of assessment where AI can't sit in for you.

But banning AI from learning isn't the answer either. Gary Eimerman's daughter had to write a "hook" for a book report. She didn't understand what a hook was. Instead of explaining it himself, Gary worked with her to ask Gemini to explain what hooks are and show examples from other books - explicitly instructing it not to give the answer. She learned what a hook was. She learned more than she would have sitting in class.

AI as a thought partner vs AI as a ghostwriter. The first builds capability. The second destroys it. And most people - most students, most professionals - default to the second because it's faster.

INSEAD's own data tells the story. Students use AI extensively across all assignments. But many aren't particularly critical of the outputs. There are situations of copy-paste that are obviously wrong. It becomes an actual ideological crutch - a substitute for thinking, not a supplement to it.

The line between using AI well and using AI lazily is the most important line in professional development right now. And almost nobody is drawing it.

07 / The Mission

What this club actually does.

01
Move people from Productive to Automation.
Not through lectures. Through doing. Workshops where you build systems, not sit through demos. Pair a first-time user with someone who's automated their workflow. Screen-share sessions showing real AI workflows. You build something every session.
02
Kill the hidden-use culture.
AI use at INSEAD feels like a dirty secret. That's backwards. This is the place where sharing how you use AI is normal. Not brave. We create the permission structure, the incentives, and the community where transparency replaces secrecy.
03
Build things that persist.
The INSEAD AI Tool Repository - a GitHub-hosted platform of student-built AI tools that carries over year to year. CV formatting, meeting transcription, assignment prep, workflow automation. Every cohort inherits what the last one built and adds to it.
04
Bridge hype and reality.
Hear from people at the cutting edge. Real use cases. Real constraints. Real failures. People who can answer "what actually went wrong when you tried to deploy this?" Not keynote speakers. Practitioners.
05
Connect both campuses.
AI adoption looks different in Asia and Europe. Different regulation, different cultural attitudes, different industries being disrupted at different speeds. A global club should leverage that, not ignore it. Co-presidents on each campus. Cross-campus build challenges.
06
Preserve learning while leveraging speed.
This is the tension nobody talks about. You can use AI and still learn - but it requires intention. We model what thoughtful AI use looks like. AI as a thought partner, not a ghostwriter. The distinction matters more than any tool.

A fool with a tool
is still a fool.

The most expensive mistake you can make with AI isn't using the wrong model. It's outsourcing the thinking that makes you valuable.

We're not an AI hype club. We're here because this technology is genuinely transformative, the disruption is already happening, and the difference between someone who navigates it well and someone who doesn't will be defined in the next two years.

This team exists to make sure INSEAD students are on the right side of that line.
